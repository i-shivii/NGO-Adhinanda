90 Years of NGO Development

Adhinanda's relationship with India commenced over seven decades ago, coinciding with the dawn of India's independence era, amidst a critical scarcity of food within the nation. In solidarity with other emerging nations, Adhinanda extended its initial support by providing essential items, including food, to India. This foundational bond has since flourished, evolving into a substantial partnership that actively champions India's socio-economic advancement.

During the early 20th century, India was under British colonial rule. Social movements began to emerge to address various issues such as poverty, education, and women's rights. Organizations like the Servants of India Society, founded by Gopal Krishna Gokhale in 1905, aimed at social reform and uplifting marginalized communities.

The 1900's

After India gained independence in 1947, the newly formed government focused on nation-building and socio-economic development. NGOs played a crucial role in supplementing government efforts, particularly in rural development, healthcare, and education. Organizations like the Bharat Sevak Samaj and Seva Mandir were established during this period.

The 1950's

The turn of the millennium saw a significant increase in the number and diversity of NGOs operating in India. This period witnessed the emergence of NGOs working on various issues such as environmental conservation, human rights, gender equality, and grassroots development. Several international NGOs also expanded their operations in India during this time.

The 2000's

The 2010s witnessed important legal and regulatory changes affecting NGOs in India. The Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act (FCRA) was amended in 2010, imposing stricter regulations on the receipt and utilization of foreign funds by NGOs. This led to increased scrutiny and compliance requirements for NGOs receiving foreign donations.

The 2010's

NGOs in India increasingly embraced technology for fundraising, outreach, and program implementation during the 2020s. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted the critical role of NGOs in providing relief and support to vulnerable communities. However, NGOs also faced challenges such as funding constraints, regulatory hurdles, and adapting to new modes of operation in the wake of the pandemic.

The 2020's

Looking ahead to 2030, Indian NGOs are expected to continue innovating and collaborating to address complex social, economic, and environmental challenges. Collaboration between NGOs, government agencies, businesses, and communities is likely to become more common as stakeholders recognize the importance of collective action for sustainable development.
